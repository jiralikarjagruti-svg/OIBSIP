01 / BIOGRAPHY

Who Was
Nikola Tesla?

Nikola Tesla was a Serbian-American inventor, electrical engineer, and futurist whose contributions to the design of the modern alternating current (AC) electricity supply system changed the course of human civilisation. Born on 10 July 1856 in Smiljan, in what is now Croatia, he displayed a remarkable aptitude for mathematics and physics from childhood.

After working briefly for Thomas Edison — before their famous falling-out over AC vs. DC power — Tesla partnered with George Westinghouse and triumphed in the legendary "War of Currents," establishing AC as the standard that powers the entire world to this day.

Tesla died penniless and largely forgotten in a New York hotel room in 1943. History has since recognised him as one of the greatest inventors and visionaries who ever lived — a man whose ideas about wireless communication, free energy, and resonance were centuries ahead of their time.

02 / TIMELINE

A Life of
Discovery

Eight milestones that changed history.

1856

Born in Smiljan, Croatia

Tesla was born into a Serbian family. From childhood he displayed a photographic memory, the ability to perform calculus mentally, and an uncanny gift for visualising machines before building them.

1882

The Vision of the Rotating Magnetic Field

While walking in a Budapest park, Tesla had a sudden vision of a rotating magnetic field — the foundational principle of AC power. He sketched the design in the dirt with a stick, and the induction motor was born.

1884

Arrives in America, Works for Edison

Tesla emigrated to the United States with four cents in his pocket and a letter of introduction to Thomas Edison. Their working relationship quickly soured over fundamental disagreements about AC versus DC power.

1888

Partnership with Westinghouse — The War of Currents

Tesla sold his AC motor patents to George Westinghouse. Edison launched a vicious public campaign against AC, even electrocuting animals to "prove" it was dangerous. Tesla and Westinghouse ultimately prevailed.

1893

Illuminating the Chicago World's Fair

Tesla and Westinghouse powered the entire Chicago World's Columbian Exposition with AC electricity — a stunning public demonstration witnessed by 27 million people that settled the War of Currents once and for all.

1899

Colorado Springs — Reaching for Space

Tesla built a massive experimental station generating millions of volts. He lit 200 lamps wirelessly from 40 km away and claimed to have received signals from outer space — decades before radio astronomy was conceived.

1901

Wardenclyffe Tower — Wireless Power for the World

Tesla began his most ambitious project: a tower designed to transmit free wireless electricity and communications to the entire globe. Funding collapsed under JP Morgan's pressure, and the unfinished tower was demolished in 1917.

1943

Death & Eternal Legacy

Tesla died alone in Room 3327 of the New Yorker Hotel. The SI unit of magnetic flux density — the Tesla (T) — was named in his honour in 1960. Today, the world's leading electric car company bears his name.

03 / ACHIEVEMENTS

The Inventions
That Power Today

Tesla's legacy is embedded in every piece of modern technology.

⚡

AC Power System

Tesla's alternating current system is the global standard for electrical transmission — used in every home, hospital, and factory on Earth.

📡

Radio Technology

Tesla demonstrated wireless signal transmission in 1893, predating Marconi. The US Supreme Court ruled in Tesla's favour in 1943 — the same year he died.

🔄

Induction Motor

The AC induction motor Tesla invented in 1887 powers electric vehicles, industrial machines, and household appliances used by billions worldwide today.

🌩️

Tesla Coil

Invented in 1891, the Tesla coil is used in radio technology, early television, medical devices, and remains a staple of science education globally.

💡

Fluorescent Lighting

Tesla developed and demonstrated fluorescent bulbs decades before they were commercialised, even creating glowing neon signs at the 1893 World's Fair.

🤖

Remote Control & Robotics

In 1898 Tesla publicly demonstrated the world's first radio-controlled vehicle — a miniature boat — planting the seed for modern robotics and drones.

04 / LEGACY

A Mind
Ahead of Time

Nikola Tesla lived and died as a misunderstood genius — robbed of credit, robbed of funding, robbed of the recognition he deserved. Yet every time you switch on a light, charge your phone, or drive an electric car, you are using technology that traces its roots directly back to him.

His life is a reminder that the greatest ideas are rarely celebrated in their own time. Tesla did not work for money or fame. He worked, as he said, for the future — and the future turned out to be ours.
